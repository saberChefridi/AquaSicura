A Brief Treatise

The Art of Hydration

A short companion, gathered from modern medicine and ancient wisdom alike, on how to drink water well — the temperature, the minerals, the posture, and the quiet discipline that turns a simple glass into a daily act of health.

Chapter I

On the Temperature of Water

Water that is too cold shocks the stomach, constricts its vessels, and slows the very digestion it was drawn to aid. Water that is too hot scalds the delicate lining of the gut. The happy mean, long recommended by physicians, is water drawn neither from ice nor from flame — but from the natural temperature of a cool cellar: somewhere between fifteen and twenty-five degrees Celsius.

At this “room temperature” the body need not expend energy to warm or to cool what it receives; the water is absorbed swiftly through the stomach wall and reaches the tissues at the pace nature intended. Upon waking, a glass of warm (never hot) water is gentler still, rousing the digestive organs without jolting them.

Cold water is a pleasure in great heat, but after exertion or with meals it is best set aside. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) is reported to have preferred cool, sweet water — not iced — and the physicians of Salerno gave the same counsel a thousand years ago.
Chapter II

On the Manner of Drinking

Modern gastroenterology and the traditions of East and West converge upon a single quiet instruction: drink slowly, and drink seated. Gulping half a litre in a single breath overwhelms the stomach, dilutes the gastric acid needed for digestion, and loads the kidneys with a sudden volume they must then hastily expel.

Sipping, by contrast, allows saliva — which is itself mildly alkaline — to mingle with the water; it gives the stomach time to signal satiety; and it permits cells to draw what they need rather than pass the excess away as urine. A small cup, refilled often, nourishes the body more deeply than a tall glass drained at once.

  • Sit down to drink. Standing compresses the stomach and sends water under pressure; seated, the organs are aligned and at rest. The Islamic tradition forbids drinking while standing, and modern studies note gentler renal and cardiac response when seated.
  • Three breaths, not one. Pause between sips. Breathe. This is an old counsel found in Arab adab and in Ayurveda alike.
  • Do not drown the meal. A small sip with food is welcome; a large glass during the meal thins digestive juices and bloats the stomach. Drink most of your water between meals.
  • Hold the glass in the right hand, as is traditional — a small courtesy to the water and to yourself.
Chapter III

Of Minerals & Living Waters

Pure water — that is, chemically pure H₂O — is not healthy water. The body requires that its drink carry the traces of the earth through which it has passed. A few dissolved minerals are not impurities but nourishment; they are what transform mere liquid into living water.

The World Health Organisation recommends a Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) of roughly 150 to 300 mg/L for pleasant, healthful drinking water. Below 100, water tastes flat and leaches minerals from the body; above 500, it may taste heavy. Within that temperate range, the following elements are the most precious:

Ca
Calcium
Builds bone and tooth; regulates heart rhythm. 40–80 mg/L is ideal.
Mg
Magnesium
Calms nerve and muscle; guards against cramp. Target 20–50 mg/L.
K
Potassium
Balances sodium; steadies blood pressure and the heartbeat.
Na
Sodium
Essential in trace — yet never above 20 mg/L in daily drinking water.
HCO₃
Bicarbonate
Gentle buffer; keeps the stomach calm and the blood faintly alkaline.
SiO₂
Silica
Quiet architect of skin, hair and connective tissue.
Water stripped to zero TDS by reverse osmosis without re-mineralisation is aggressive water: it will readily take minerals from the very body it is meant to sustain. Every AquaSicura system therefore concludes with a mineral re-injection stage — the final breath of life returned to the filtered stream.
Chapter IV

On the Rhythm of the Day

A healthy adult needs roughly two litres of water a day — more in heat, exertion or illness. Yet more important than the sum is the rhythm by which it is taken. Drink a little, often, through the waking hours, and the body receives a steady river rather than a tide.

  • On waking. One glass of warm water, sipped slowly, before anything else. It stirs the digestive fire gently and rehydrates the blood thickened by the night.
  • Half an hour before meals. A glass primes the stomach and sharpens the sense of true hunger.
  • Between meals. Small sips every twenty or thirty minutes. This is the body’s preferred rhythm.
  • After exertion. Cool — not iced — water, sipped, never gulped.
  • Evening. Taper the quantity an hour before sleep, that the kidneys too may rest.
Chapter V

A Closing Counsel

Hydration is the quietest of the disciplines. It asks no equipment, no appointment, no effort — only attention. Drink water that is clean, mineralised and alive; drink it seated, sipped and warmed to the temperament of the body; drink it through the whole of the day, rather than all at once. Do this, and one of the humblest habits of the household becomes, in time, one of the surest foundations of a long and luminous life.

A Private Gallery

Water in Art

The painter, like the poet, has always returned to water. Waves and ponds, storms and still lagoons — eight of the great canvases in which water is not background, but the true subject: living, breathing, untamed.

Met Museum · Edo Period
Plate I

The Great Wave off Kanagawa

Katsushika Hokusai · c. 1831

The most celebrated wave ever drawn. Hokusai’s claw-like curl, rising over three slender fishing boats with Mount Fuji impossibly distant beyond it, has become the very emblem of the ocean’s sublime indifference to men and mountains alike — a woodblock print that travelled the world and changed Western art forever.

Russian Museum · Saint Petersburg
Plate II

The Ninth Wave

Ivan Aivazovsky · 1850

Survivors cling to a shattered mast at the dawn after a storm. The Armenian-Russian master, unrivalled painter of seas, renders the water as a living being: luminous, terrible, and somehow — in the warm gold of the rising sun — merciful. An old sailor’s belief names the ninth wave the most fearful; Aivazovsky paints it as the last.

Musée de l’Orangerie · Paris
Plate III

Water Lilies — The Clouds

Claude Monet · 1914–1926

In the garden pond at Giverny Monet found a world sufficient for the last thirty years of his life. The surface is neither water nor sky but both at once: reflection dissolves matter, and the viewer stands suspended between the floating lilies and the clouds beneath them. The great Nymphéas cycle is the quietest monument of Impressionism.

National Gallery · London
Plate IV

The Fighting Téméraire

J. M. W. Turner · 1839

A ghost-white ship-of-the-line, hero of Trafalgar, is drawn across still estuary water by a dark tug to be broken up for scrap; the sun sets behind her in gold and crimson. Turner’s elegy for the age of sail is carried by the Thames itself — a river turned mirror of a disappearing world. Often named Britain’s favourite painting.

Metropolitan Museum of Art · New York
Plate V

The Gulf Stream

Winslow Homer · 1899

A dismasted sloop, a lone Black sailor, circling sharks, a distant schooner and an approaching waterspout — Homer’s American masterpiece is unsparing and magnificent. The sea here is power, fate, and a vast unjudged beauty. Hemingway read this painting as much as any book when he wrote The Old Man and the Sea.

Alte Nationalgalerie · Berlin
Plate VI

The Monk by the Sea

Caspar David Friedrich · 1808–1810

A single small figure stands on a dune before an immensity of sea and sky. No horizon line, no ship, no comfort — only water, air, and a human silhouette no larger than a thumbprint. Friedrich’s Romantic meditation on solitude and the infinite is, in truth, a painting of how small we are, and how beautifully so.

Musée d’Orsay · Paris
Plate VII

The Wave

Gustave Courbet · 1869

Courbet painted the Norman coast again and again, building his waves with the palette knife in thick, almost sculpted strokes. This close, low view of a single cresting wave — the beach empty, the horizon heavy with cloud — is the sea without ornament: muscular, lonely, entirely itself. Cézanne called it the finest wave ever painted.

Uffizi Gallery · Florence
Plate VIII

The Birth of Venus

Sandro Botticelli · c. 1485

From the foam of the Aegean, upon a great scallop shell, beauty is born into the world. Botticelli’s Renaissance allegory is also, quietly, a painting about water: the sea as origin, as mother, as the first cradle of life. The gentle waves under Venus’ shell were, for a Florentine poet-painter, nothing less than the beginning of all things.

“In every drop of water there is a story of life.”— A closing meditation

The Process

The Science of Living Water

Eight precision stages. One proprietary secret. The result is not filtered water — it is revived water.

01

Stage One

Pre-Filtration

Water enters the system through a multi-stage sediment barrier. Particles, rust, and suspended solids down to 5 microns are captured before treatment begins — protecting the precision stages that follow.

02

Stage Two

Activated Carbon Filtration

A dense bed of granular activated carbon — produced from coconut shell — adsorbs chlorine, chloramines, herbicides, and dissolved organic compounds. Taste and odour are erased. The carbon's microscopic pore structure provides millions of square centimetres of contact surface per gram.

03

Stage Three

Reverse Osmosis

At the heart of the system, a semi-permeable membrane with pores of 0.0001 micron rejects up to 99% of dissolved salts, heavy metals, bacteria, viruses, and pharmaceutical residues. Only pure water molecules pass through. The concentrate is cleanly discharged. Nothing harmful remains.

04

Stage Four

UV Sterilisation

Post-membrane water passes through a calibrated UV-C chamber. Ultraviolet light at 254 nm disrupts the DNA of any surviving microorganism — eliminating pathogens without chemistry, without residue, without altering the water's mineral balance in any way.

05

Stage Five

Thermal Exchange

A compact heat-exchange module brings the water to the ideal service temperature — chilled, ambient, or gently warm — using energy recovered from within the system itself. Temperature is not merely comfort: it is part of the sensory and physiological experience of water.
